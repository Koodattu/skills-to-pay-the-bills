# Format Guide

Channel conventions. The core principles (claim first, specifics, asymmetric structure, no slop) apply everywhere; this file covers what each form additionally rewards. Read only the section you need.

## Blog post / essay / article

- 600–2,000 words for most; long only when the material forces it.
- Hook: a concrete problem, surprising number, contrarian claim, or scene from experience. Within two paragraphs the reader knows the claim and why they should care.
- Body: claims in the order the reader needs them, each carried by an example, number, or named case. Sections sized by importance.
- Include one section of counterpressure: where this fails, what it costs, what smart people get wrong, with a verdict.
- End on the implication or the sharpened claim. No summary.
- Title: specific promise or claim, not a topic label. "How we cut CI time from 40 to 9 minutes" beats "Optimizing CI pipelines." Avoid colon-subtitle-listicle patterns unless the channel demands them.
- Silent pre-flight: What is the reader trying to solve or decide? What's the promise? What's the non-obvious angle? What can be cut because the reader already knows it? What objection must be answered? What should survive one minute of memory?

## Technical writing (docs, explainers, engineering blog posts, RFCs, READMEs)

- State what the thing is and who it's for in the first two sentences. No mission-statement preamble.
- Accuracy over elegance: exact version numbers, exact commands, exact error messages. Never approximate something checkable.
- Show, then explain: a working example before the abstraction. Code blocks tested or marked untested.
- Structure by reader task (install, configure, debug), not by system architecture, unless the audience is contributors.
- Admit sharp edges. "This breaks if X" is the most valuable sentence in a doc; AI-flavored docs omit failure modes.
- READMEs: what it does, who needs it, minimal working example, install, link out for the rest. Badge walls and feature-adjective lists are slop.
- RFCs/design docs: the decision and its main tradeoff up front; alternatives considered with real reasons for rejection, not strawmen.

## Newsletter

- One idea per issue. Subject line makes a specific promise the first paragraph keeps.
- Voice is the product: first person, opinions owned, consistent persona across issues.
- Length: as short as the idea allows. Respect the inbox.
- End with one clear next thing (a link, a question, a takeaway), not three.

## Email (professional)

- The ask or the news in the first two sentences. Context after, and only what's needed to act.
- One email, one topic. Multiple asks get numbered so none is lost.
- Subject line states content, not vibe: "Q3 budget: need sign-off by Friday."
- Cut throat-clearing openers ("I hope this finds you well") unless the relationship or culture expects them.

## Landing page / product copy

- Headline: the outcome for the customer, concrete. Not the category, not the mission.
- Every claim gets proof within eyeshot: number, named customer, screenshot, demo.
- Features translated to outcomes, but skip the formula "X so that you can Y" repeated down the page; vary construction.
- Slop vocabulary is fatal here (seamless, robust, revolutionize, all-in-one). Say what it does in the words a customer would use complaining about the old way.
- One primary call to action per page.

## Social posts (LinkedIn, X, etc.)

- First line does all the work; write it last, after the point is clear.
- One idea, one post. Threads only when each post stands alone.
- Plain text. No hashtags unless the platform's discovery genuinely depends on them. No engagement-bait formulas ("Agree?").
- Short lines and white space are fine on these platforms; the slop tell is the formula (hook, "here's the thing," triad, "not X but Y," CTA), not the line breaks.
- Data or a specific story beats an opinion restated. "We A/B tested this across 40k sends: plain-text won by 23%" is a post; "authenticity matters" is not.

## Report / memo

- Bottom line up front: conclusion and recommendation in the first paragraph, always.
- Findings ordered by decision-relevance, not by chronology of the work.
- Numbers carry the argument; prose interprets only where interpretation is non-obvious.
- One-page summary rule: if the reader stops after page one, they should still make the right decision.

## Repurposing between formats

Re-derive, don't compress. A blog post becoming a LinkedIn post keeps the single sharpest claim and one piece of evidence, then rebuilds the opening for a feed context. A talk becoming an article gains the specifics that gesture and slides carried. Ask again: who is this reader, what do they already know, what's the one-minute takeaway here?
