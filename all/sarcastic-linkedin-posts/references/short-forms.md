# Short Forms: Comments and DMs

Posts are monologues; comments and DMs are duets. The other party's content is your straight man — the satire comes from how you respond to it, so mirror its specifics. Generic sarcasm in a reply is a wasted turn.

Both forms are SHORT. A comment past ~80 words stops being a comment and becomes a hijack (which is a legitimate archetype — but choose it deliberately). A DM past ~150 words is a post wearing an envelope.

---

## Comments

### The five comment archetypes (pick exactly one)

1. **The Deadpan Affirmation** — the "Great post!" reply, played so straight it curdles. Works best under absurd or hollow posts: agreeing enthusiastically with nonsense is the purest form of the genre.

   > Under a post about a CEO answering emails during his own wedding:
   > "This is the standard. Congratulations to your wife on marrying into such strong response times, Greg."

2. **The One-Upper Mini-Broem** — a reply that hijacks the post with the commenter's own, slightly more extreme story. The signature move: open by honoring the post, then make it about you within one line.

   > "Powerful, Sandra. This reminds me of the time I closed a Series A from a hospital bed. The nurses said rest. The term sheet said otherwise. We all have our journey 🙏"

3. **The Hedged Disagreement** — LinkedIn's only permitted conflict format: grovel, agree, then disagree so gently the disagreement is undetectable.

   > "Great points, and I don't disagree with a single word — genuinely brilliant post. I'd just gently add that the exact opposite is also true. Both things can be false. Thanks for starting this conversation 👏"

4. **The Thread-Hijack Pitch** — the sales comment. Compliment → pivot → funnel, in under 30 words.

   > "Love this energy! Speaking of resilience — I help founders monetize their trauma. Sent you a DM 🚀"

5. **The Pod Voice** — the suspiciously instant, suspiciously template-shaped engagement-pod comment. The tell IS the joke: first-name usage, zero content specificity, posted 40 seconds after publication.
   > "So many golden nuggets here, [FirstName]! Saving this one. You always deliver 💯"
   > (Keep the literal "[FirstName]" placeholder if the mode is Savage — the unfilled template is the punchline.)

### Comment craft rules

- **One joke.** Comments don't have room for an escalation ladder. Set up, detonate, stop.
- **Quote or mirror one specific detail** from the post being replied to. Specificity is what separates satire from a canned reply.
- **First-name the poster** — the genre's false-intimacy signature.
- **No hashtags** (bot tell), unless writing Pod Voice where bot-tell is the point.
- **Emoji budget: 0–2.** A single 🙏 or 💯 lands harder than a row.
- **Punch at the reply culture, not the poster** — unless the user explicitly wants their own invented post roasted. When replying to a real post the user pasted, mock the genre the post belongs to, not the human who wrote it.

## DMs and DM Replies

Two sub-forms with different mechanics.

### Cold DM (outgoing parody)

Satirizes the automated-outreach genre: personalization theater, manufactured urgency, the calendar link. Structure:

1. **The fake-personal opener** — reference something from the target's profile in a way that proves the sender read nothing. "I came across your profile and was blown away by your experience in [INDUSTRY]."
2. **The unearned rapport** — "I feel like we're very aligned" (based on zero interactions).
3. **The pitch disguised as curiosity** — "Quick question:" followed by a question that is not quick and answers itself.
4. **Fake scarcity** — "I only work with 3 founders per quarter, and one spot just opened up (long story 😅)."
5. **The calendar link** — the true payload. Satirically: "Grab 15 minutes here whenever works (I've held Tuesday for you)."
6. **The follow-up threat** — optional closer: "If I don't hear back, I'll circle back Thursday. And Friday. I believe in follow-through."

Example (Deadpan mode):

> Hi Marcus! I came across your profile and was genuinely inspired by your journey in [industry]. Not many people can say they've done what you've done (I would say it, but LinkedIn character limits, haha).
>
> Quick question — are you happy with your current growth trajectory, or are you open to being happy?
>
> I only take on 3 partners a quarter and something tells me you're a 3-partners-a-quarter kind of leader.
>
> Grabbed us a slot Tuesday at 2. If that doesn't work, no pressure at all — I'll simply follow up every 48 hours until it does.
>
> To your success,
> Brayden

### DM Reply (responding to a message the user received)

The most requested real-world use: the user pastes recruiter spam, a sales pitch, or "let's connect!" boilerplate and wants a sarcastic reply. Rules:

- **Match their energy, then exceed it by 15%.** If they used three exclamation points, use four. If they called the role a "rocket ship," ask about the seating chart on the rocket.
- **Take their template literally.** The core move: respond to the form letter as if it were sincere and personal. Unfilled placeholders, wrong names, and mismatched industries are gifts — engage with them earnestly. ("You mentioned my 'impressive background in [SECTOR]' — thank you. [SECTOR] has given me so much.")
- **Interrogate one specific claim.** Pick the most inflated phrase in their message ("fast-paced environment," "competitive compensation," "like a family") and ask sincere, escalating clarifying questions about it. ("You say the team is like a family. Whose? Is there mediation?")
- **The deadpan yes** — alternatively, accept enthusiastically at an absurd level of literalness, with conditions. ("I'd love to hop on a quick call. My rate for quick calls is $340. Quarter-hour minimum. Tuesday works.")
- **Stay send-able-ish.** The best replies are ones the user could actually send: funny, pointed at the message's genre, containing nothing abusive toward the individual sender. The sender is a fellow hostage of the format — the format is the target.
- **No hashtags, no engagement bait** — this is private. The comedy of DMs is intimacy-shaped corporate speak.

Example — replying to "Hi! Your background is a great fit for an exciting opportunity at a fast-growing startup! Open to a quick chat?":

> Hi Jordan! Thrilled you found my background a great fit — which part specifically? Take your time. I'll wait.
>
> I'm absolutely open to a quick chat about the exciting opportunity. Before we book it, just a few quick questions of my own: how fast is the startup growing, in what direction, and is anyone steering?
>
> Also, when you say "fast-paced environment," could you give me that in a number? Meetings per day is fine. Blood pressure also accepted.
>
> Looking forward to circling back before you do,
> [Name]

## Failure modes specific to short forms

1. **The comment that's secretly a post** — 200 words with a moral and hashtags. Fix: one joke, cut to ≤80 words.
2. **Ignoring the source material** — a reply that could sit under any post/message. Fix: mirror at least one specific phrase from what you're replying to.
3. **Punching at the sender instead of the template** — calling the recruiter an idiot isn't satire, it's just rude, and it's not send-able. Fix: treat their message as the genre's fault; engage the human with weaponized politeness.
4. **Over-emoji** — short forms magnify emoji density. Fix: 0–2 in comments, 0–3 in DMs.
5. **Winking** — same rule as posts, less room to recover. In ≤80 words, a single "lol" is 100% of your credibility.
