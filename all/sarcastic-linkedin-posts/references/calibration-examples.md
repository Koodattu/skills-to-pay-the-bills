# Calibration Examples

One premise — "I dropped my phone in the toilet" — executed in all four modes, with annotations. Use these to calibrate tone, not to copy.

---

## Mode 1: Deadpan (default, the masterful one)

> This morning, I dropped my phone in the toilet.
>
> Most people would call that a bad start to the day.
>
> I call it the best offsite I've had all year.
>
> For 3 hours and 41 minutes, I was unreachable.
>
> No Slack. No email. No "quick syncs."
>
> And in that silence, I finally heard the one stakeholder I'd been ignoring for years:
>
> Myself.
>
> I took a meeting with him. He had concerns.
>
> We aligned on next steps.
>
> The phone survived, by the way. Rice works.
>
> But I didn't come back the same.
>
> Disconnection isn't downtime. It's due diligence on your own life.
>
> When was the last time YOU dropped your phone in a toilet — on purpose or otherwise? 👇
>
> #mindfulness #leadership #deepwork #growthmindset #digitalwellness

**Why it works:**

- First three lines pass as a real post. The absurdity arrives on schedule, not on line one.
- One killer detail: "3 hours and 41 minutes" — non-round, fake-precise.
- The pivot is disproportionate (toilet → offsite) but delivered with total confidence.
- "I took a meeting with him. He had concerns." — the comedy lives in a five-word line, deadpan corporate voice applied to introspection. No wink.
- The moral is quotable and hollow: "due diligence on your own life."
- The bait question is exactly as inane as real bait questions.
- Note what it does NOT do: no "lol," no acknowledgment, no piling on hustle-porn AND humblebrag AND founder-story. Trope budget respected: stranger-encounter energy (the stranger is himself) + fake vulnerability. Two archetypes.

## Mode 2: Escalating Absurdist

> This morning, I dropped my phone in the toilet.
>
> I didn't fish it out.
>
> I watched it sink, and I thought about legacy.
>
> By 9 AM I had cancelled my meetings.
>
> By noon I had cancelled my phone plan.
>
> By 3 PM I was explaining to my wife that we, as a family, were "sunsetting connectivity."
>
> She asked if I was okay. I asked her to take it offline.
>
> Day 4: I now communicate exclusively through handwritten memos, delivered by my son (7), who I've onboarded as Chief of Staff. His KPIs are age-appropriate.
>
> Day 9: Productivity is up 340%. I no longer know what my job is. This is what founders call "zooming out."
>
> The phone is still in the toilet.
>
> It has become a monument.
>
> Some people flush their potential. I flushed my distractions.
>
> Repost ♻️ if you'd hire my son.
>
> #futureofwork #familyoffice #unplugged #operationalexcellence

**Why it works:**

- Same plausible opening; the escalation ladder is even (each beat ~15% more unhinged than the last), and the narrator's serenity never cracks.
- "I asked her to take it offline" — corporate idiom colliding with marriage is the mode's core move.
- The 7-year-old Chief of Staff with age-appropriate KPIs is the one killer detail; everything after coasts on it.
- Ends past the ending: the repost bait recruits the reader into the delusion.

## Mode 3: Savage

> I dropped my phone in the toilet and my first thought — my FIRST thought — was:
>
> "This is content."
>
> Not "grab it." Not "gross."
>
> "This is content."
>
> That's what this platform has done to us.
>
> There are people on here right now turning their grandmother's funeral into a carousel about customer retention.
>
> Somewhere, a man is watching his marriage collapse and thinking: hook, three lessons, soft CTA.
>
> We didn't build personal brands. We built personal brand prisons, and the algorithm is the warden, and the rent is one vulnerable childhood memory per week.
>
> Anyway.
>
> Here's what my toilet phone taught me about B2B pipeline velocity:
>
> 1. Momentum matters
> 2. Some leads should stay flushed
> 3. Always have a backup device
>
> Comment "FLUSH" and I'll send you the framework.
>
> #authenticity

**Why it works:**

- The mask comes off — the target is the incentive structure, not a person — but the post still speaks fluent LinkedIn (hook, repetition, "Read that again" cadence).
- The turn at "Anyway." is the whole joke: after the indictment, the narrator commits the crime, because the funnel always wins.
- One hashtag: #authenticity. Restraint as punchline.
- Note the punching direction: at the genre and the platform economy. No real people, no real grief mocked — the funeral and marriage are hypothetical archetypes, kept abstract.

## Mode 4: Self-Aware

> Yes, this is a LinkedIn post about dropping my phone in the toilet.
>
> Yes, there will be a lesson.
>
> No, I am not okay, but that's a different post (scheduled for Thursday).
>
> The phone fell. I lunged. I missed. For one instant, suspended between porcelain and destiny, it showed me my own reflection in the black screen.
>
> Talk about a performance review.
>
> Here's the thing:
>
> I could tell you this taught me presence, or letting go, or that the real 5G was inside us all along.
>
> But we both know why we're here.
>
> Engagement is engagement.
>
> Agree? Disagree? Either way, the algorithm thanks you for your service.
>
> #content #thisiscontent #toiletleadership

**Why it works:**

- Gets exactly ONE wink budget — spent on the opening confession — then still commits to the form (the lunge scene is played straight).
- "scheduled for Thursday" — the killer detail: even the breakdown is a content calendar.
- The honesty about engagement is itself engagement bait, which the post knows, which the reader knows the post knows. That regress is the joke.

## Common failure modes (what "generic sarcastic" looks like — avoid)

1. **The checklist post** — every trope crammed in: 4 AM alarm AND cold plunge AND fired-then-founded AND crying in a car AND Forbes. Reads as a Mad Lib. Fix: pick 2–3 archetypes, cut the rest.
2. **Winking mid-post** — "(yes, really lol)" or "cringe, I know." Kills deadpan instantly. Fix: delete; trust the reader.
3. **Front-loaded absurdity** — opening with "My goldfish taught me M&A strategy" spends the twist in line one. Fix: open plausible, escalate.
4. **Round numbers** — "100 rejections," "$1M." Real lunatics use fake precision. Fix: 47 rejections, $1.3M ARR, 6:47 AM.
5. **Explaining the moral** — following the fortune cookie with a paragraph unpacking it. Fix: the moral stands alone, hollow and proud.
6. **Mean instead of sharp** — mocking a named person, a real company's layoffs, or the reader. Fix: aim at the format, the incentives, the performance — the genre is the villain, and it's a rich one.
7. **Too long** — sarcasm has a half-life. If the post scrolls twice, it's a bit past the bit. Fix: cut 10–20%; end on the second punchline (hashtags/P.S.), not the fourth.
